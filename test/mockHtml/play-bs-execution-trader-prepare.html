Context

Looking to the future, buy-side trading desks will be highly automated and driven by AI. Buy-side traders will increasingly focus on automating trading processes to reduce manual error, improve execution performance, and free up limited bandwidth, particularly when trading multiple products across multiple brokers. They will reserve manual processes for things like exception management, critical decision making and manual handling of the difficult orders.

Advances in automation mean buy-side firms have more technical criteria for selecting sell-side dealers, including measuring hit-rates, execution quality, and other performance statistics. Following in the footsteps of equities, the electronification of primary bond markets is the new frontier for automation. This includes the grey market for bonds to trade in the hours between the announcement and subsequent pricing/allocation.

Most asset managers are consolidating their trading desks into centralised platforms with multi-asset trading capablities. The modern trading desk at larger buy sides may be split into cash, derivatives or asset class teams. That means there will typically be a cash equities trading team, that will also handle ETFs.

There will be a fixed income trading team that handles cash bonds. Sometimes there will be a another trading team that handles all types of derivatives, be that exchange-traded or over the counter. As such, there's a growing need for workflows and technology to support this.

Buy-side traders are increasingly using data-driven tools to source liquidity and provide insights to share with portfolio managers. Liquidity is particularly variable in the cash credit markets. Previous crises show that liquidity can evaporate incredibly quickly. That means pre-trade liquidity checks need to be incorporated into any investment strategy devised.

Figuring out probabilities, like time horizons and the cost of executing trades, has become increasingly important. Consolidating all quotes received via any tool - be that chat, RUNZ, IOIs, RFQ platforms, e-mails or other means of communication - is becoming fundamental to gauge the chances and timing of execution.

The buy-side industry has seen a reduction in fees as a result of consolidation, low-cost passive investing and new tech-heavy entrants. Cutting and optimizing costs are top of mind, including consolidation of systems. Following MiFID's radical changes, the consumption of external research has become more explicitly expensive as buy sides exposed to Europe's regulations must now pay for it.

Consequently, there is pressure on traders to capitalize on internal research and trade ideas. Bloomberg's RMS solutions can help clients track who is consuming what research. Also to boost efficiency, the lines between credit and equity research are increasingly overlapping, which means co-operation between the two separate departments is rising as they collaborate.

Connect

2024 is the year of “more”: more elections, more news, more uncertainty. Known client challenges include too much information and too little time, difficulty cutting through noise and generating meaningful insight quickly, consistency of data and putting data into context, and discoverability of content due to fragmentation of technology.

Clients face the challenge of deciding whether falling inflation suggests a “soft landing” for global economies, or a recession; Central Bank pivots will cause volatility as market participants try to anticipate timing of cuts.

And with more elections than ever before in 2024 and outcomes harder to call, customers face difficulty monitoring opinion and modelling outcomes. New risks can accelerate quickly. Finding trusted data and staying close to predictions and opinions is challenging.

The rally in equity markets has not lifted all stocks equally and there are now wide variations in valuations across sectors and regions. Clients will need to know where the big outliers are now and how to dig deeper into specific stocks they are screening.

Credit spreads are currently historically tight, but market sentiment is expecting credit default rates to rise due to the macro environment. Evaluating relative value across sectors and issuers is increasingly important. Sharp moves in government bonds have left some markets distorted. Tracking and explaining these dislocations is also important.

As the US begins to cut rates, there could be potential capital movement from USD to undervalued currencies, causing them to outperform USD. Due to the rate cutting environment and geopolitical events, FX volatility will increase.

2024’s expected cut in central bank rates will likely create a trading tilt, i.e. everyone will want to be long bonds. Any pick-up in defaults or credit downgrades could see spreads widen so issuer selection and liquidity will become more important.

Sharp moves in government bonds have left some markets increasingly distorted; tracking and understanding these dislocations is very important.

With markets full of volatility, fast hedging decisions to secure the best prices with efficient execution are key to protecting against downside risk.
